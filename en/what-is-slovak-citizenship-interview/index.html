What is the Slovak Citizenship Interview?

Slovak Citizenship · Guide

If you are applying for Slovak citizenship, in the vast majority of cases you will face an interview before a commission (in Slovak, pohovor). This is not a formality — it is a separate, legally mandated step that determines whether the Ministry of the Interior will advance your application. This article explains what the interview covers, how it is conducted, and how it is assessed.

Legal basis

The interview is governed by § 8(6) of Act No. 40/1993 Coll. (Z. z.) on the Citizenship of the Slovak Republic. Under this provision, the applicant must demonstrate that they command the Slovak language in both spoken and written form and possess a basic knowledge of Slovak civic and cultural realities. In other words, the exam tests two things at once: language and content knowledge. Being strong in only one of them is not enough.

The three components of the interview

In practice, the interview consists of three parts, all conducted on the same day in the order determined by the commission:

1. Oral questioning (content knowledge)

The commission asks questions covering Slovak history, geography, constitutional framework, culture, and everyday life. You answer orally, in Slovak. Assessment covers both factual accuracy and the ability to express ideas clearly.

2. Reading an article aloud (language — pronunciation)

You are given a printed Slovak newspaper article (typically 500 words or more). Without preparation, you read it aloud. The commission listens for fluency, stress, pausing, and comprehension of the content.

3. Written summary of the article (language — writing)

After reading, you write a summary of the same article in Slovak. You have 30 minutes and you write by hand. Assessment covers spelling, grammar, vocabulary, and whether you captured the main ideas of the text.

Who sits on the commission

The interview is conducted by a three-member commission, appointed by the relevant district office at the regional capital (in Slovak, okresný úrad v sídle kraja). They are typically civil servants with relevant qualifications; at least one member specialises in the language component. Their purpose is not to "catch you out," but to determine whether you can genuinely function in a Slovak-speaking environment — read the news, understand an official letter, articulate your own view.

Where and when the interview takes place

The interview takes place at the district office at the regional capital (okresný úrad v sídle kraja) corresponding to your place of permanent residence — Bratislava, Trnava, Trenčín, Nitra, Žilina, Banská Bystrica, Prešov, or Košice. The precise date and time are communicated to you by the office in a written invitation (pozvánka), usually several weeks in advance. The complete interview typically lasts 90 to 120 minutes, including the 30-minute written summary.

How the result is assessed

The result is binary — passed or failed. There are no points, no "satisfactory" grade. The commission evaluates all three components together and produces a written record, which becomes part of your application file. If you clearly fail one component — for instance, produce no written summary at all, or cannot read the article aloud — the commission is unlikely to conclude that you command Slovak "in both spoken and written form," and the outcome will be a fail.

What happens if I fail?

A failed interview is not the end of the process. You can request a retake after a certain period (typically not sooner than six months), giving you time to address the gaps. However, the Ministry will examine your second attempt more carefully, looking for evidence of genuine progress. For this reason it is wise to go into the first interview only when you are truly ready — not "to give it a try."

What the interview tests — and what it does not

It is important to understand what the commission does not examine:

  • It does not test academic Slovak. You are not expected to recite all grammatical cases in order. You are expected to use Slovak practically and competently.
  • It does not test encyclopaedic knowledge. You do not need to memorise the exact dates of every battle. You do need a working grasp of the key events and figures of Slovak history.
  • It does not test your political views. The focus is on knowledge and language — not on whether you support a particular party or political direction.

Summary

The Slovak citizenship interview is a serious but fair examination. It has clear rules, clear components, and clear objectives. Someone who arrived in Slovakia years before applying, lives here, speaks Slovak, and takes an interest in the country whose citizen they wish to become has good prospects of passing.
