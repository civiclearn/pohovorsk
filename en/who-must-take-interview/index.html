Who Must Take the Interview, and Who Is Exempt?

Slovak Citizenship · Guide

Before you invest months in preparation, it is worth confirming whether the interview actually applies to you. The Slovak citizenship law specifies which categories of applicants are required to take it, and which are exempt. This article will help you orient yourself. It is a general overview; your specific situation can only be formally assessed by the relevant district office (okresný úrad) or by the Ministry of the Interior of the Slovak Republic.

The basic rule

If you are applying for Slovak citizenship under § 7 of Act No. 40/1993 Coll., you are required to take the interview — unless a specifically enumerated statutory exemption applies to you. The office does not consider your nationality or country of origin when making this determination; it looks at the legal basis on which you are applying, and whether any exemption condition is met.

Who must take the interview

In the overwhelming majority of cases, the following applicant categories apply:

Standard applicant after long-term residence

A person applying after the statutory period of continuous residence (typically 8 years). This is the most common category, and the interview is mandatory for it.

Spouse of a Slovak citizen

Marriage to a Slovak citizen reduces the required residence period but does not exempt the spouse from the interview. The spouse of a citizen must still demonstrate both language proficiency and knowledge of Slovak civic matters.

Applicant under special interest of the Slovak Republic

Persons applying under § 7(2) (exceptional interest of the Slovak Republic) are also generally required to take the interview, unless the Ministry explicitly waives it. No exemption is automatic.

Persons granted asylum, after their residence period

After the reduced residence period has elapsed, the interview requirement applies to persons granted asylum as well — in the standard format, under the same rules as everyone else.

Who is exempt from the interview

The law lists several categories for whom the interview is waived — either due to age or health status, or because the applicant has already demonstrated knowledge of Slovak in another relevant way. The most common cases are:

Children under 14

Minor children do not take the interview. In applications filed jointly by a whole family, only the adult applicant takes the interview; the child does not.

Persons with a health condition preventing the interview

If a health condition objectively prevents you from taking the interview, you may request an exemption. A medical certificate must be attached to the application; the Ministry makes the final determination.

Graduates of Slovak schools

If you have completed at least three years of study at a Slovak primary, secondary, or tertiary school with Slovak as the language of instruction, the office may exempt you from the interview — the school diploma is accepted as evidence of language competence.

Holders of a Slovak state language exam

A certificate of the state language examination in Slovak, issued by a state language school in the Slovak Republic, is generally recognised. In practice this covers the language component; the content knowledge component may still be required.

Former Czech citizens (special transitional regimes)

Historically, special transitional provisions relating to the dissolution of Czechoslovakia apply to citizens of the Czech Republic. This regime can change — the current state of affairs can be confirmed by the district office.

Important: no exemption is automatic. One must always be requested in writing and substantiated with the relevant evidence (diploma, certificate, medical report). The Ministry decides on a case-by-case basis and is entitled to request supplementary documentation.

How to determine whether the interview applies to you

  1. Identify the provision under which you are applying. Standard applications are filed under § 7. There are also special bases — for example, § 7(2) or the restoration of citizenship under § 9.
  2. Go through the list of exemptions. If you fall into any of the categories above, prepare the supporting documents before filing the application.
  3. Contact the district office at the capital of your region. A precise assessment of your situation can only come from the office that will handle your file. An informal consultation can save months.
  4. Prepare as if the interview were mandatory. Until you have a written exemption decision in hand, assume the interview will take place. That way you will be ready even if the office denies your exemption request.

Summary

In the vast majority of cases, applicants are required to take the interview. Exemptions do exist, but they are not automatic and generally relate to age, health, or language competence already demonstrated within the Slovak educational system. The safest strategy is to clarify your status as early as possible and — if you fall within the standard applicant categories — to begin preparing systematically and with time to spare.
